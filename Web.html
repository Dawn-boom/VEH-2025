Multiple Spherical Magnets-Based Annular Electromagnetic Energy Harvester

Introduction

As the imbalance between energy supply and demand grows increasingly critical, energy harvesting has emerged as a promising way to alleviate the problem. In this paper, an annular electromagnetic energy harvester based on spherical magnets is introduced, which is suited for harvesting energy from rotating structures such as wheels under various scenarios. MATLAB and COMSOL simulation were conducted respectively to model the magnetic distribution of spherical magnets and the magnetic line cutting motion between magnets and coils. Experiments were conducted to test the harvester's performance, with a variation of coil counts from 1 to 5, and the number of magnets from 2 to 6 under a self-built test system. According to the results, our device can achieve a maximum peak power of 23 mW, and charge the 100 μF, 230 μF and 330 μF capacitors to 1.75 V, 1.625 V, and 1.275 V in 180 seconds respectively, showing a promising prospect in solving energy problems.

Model Design

Our design features a toroidal design with an inclined cut to reduce friction, with coils tightly wound around the structure and magnetic beads arranged end-to-end inside the inner tube, ensuring sliding rather than rolling motion, as shown in Fig. 1. Based on the principle of electromagnetic induction, electric current is generated as the tube rotates and the coils cut through the magnetic flux lines, efficiently converting mechanical motion into electrical energy.

Theory Model

Demagnetizing Field of a Spherical Magnet

By solving the Laplace's equation, the magnetic potential inside the uniformly magnetized sphere is given by:

\[ \varphi_i = - \mathbf{H}_i \cdot \mathbf{r}, \] (1)

where \(\mathbf{H}_i\) is the magnetic field inside the sphere, and \(\mathbf{r}\) is the position vector from the center of the sphere.


Outside the sphere, the magnetic potential is expressed as:

\[ \varphi_e = - \mathbf{H}_0 \cdot \mathbf{r} + \frac{\mathbf{m} \cdot \mathbf{r}}{r^3}, \] (2)

where \(\mathbf{H}_0\) is the external magnetic field, \(\mathbf{m}\) is the magnetic dipole moment, and \(r = |\mathbf{r}|\) is the distance from the sphere's center.


The magnetic dipole moment is given by:

\[ \mathbf{m} = \frac{4\pi}{3} a^3 \mathbf{M}, \] (3)

where \(a\) is the sphere's radius, and \(\mathbf{M}\) is the uniform magnetization of the sphere.


To determine \(\mathbf{M}\) and \(\mathbf{H}_i\), boundary conditions at the sphere's surface are applied:

  • The normal component of magnetic induction is continuous:
    \[ B_{i,\perp} = B_{e,\perp}. \] (4)
  • The tangential component of magnetic field strength is continuous:
    \[ H_{i,\parallel} = H_{e,\parallel}. \] (5)

Using these boundary conditions, the magnetization vector is derived as:

\[ \mathbf{M} = \frac{3(\mu_i - \mu_e)}{\mu_i + 2\mu_e} \mathbf{H}_0 + \frac{3\mu_0 \mathbf{M}_0}{\mu_i + 2\mu_e}, \] (6)

where \(\mu_i\) and \(\mu_e\) are the magnetic permeabilities of the sphere and the surrounding medium, respectively.


Then the magnetic field inside the sphere is expressed as:

\[ \mathbf{H}_i = \frac{3\mu_e}{\mu_i + 2\mu_e} \mathbf{H}_0 - \frac{\mu_0 \mathbf{M}_0}{\mu_i + 2\mu_e}, \] (7)

where \(\mathbf{M}_0\) represents the sphere's intrinsic magnetization.


The demagnetizing field \(\mathbf{H}_M\) created by the sphere as a result of its magnetization (induced and permanent) is defined as:

\[ \mathbf{H}_M = \mathbf{H}_i-\mathbf{H}_0 = - \frac{\mathbf{M}}{3}. \] (8)

Magnetic Field of a Single Magnetic Bead

Assuming that the spherical magnet is uniformly magnetized. Then, according to Eq. (8), the magnetic field intensity inside a uniformly magnetized sphere \( \mathbf{H} \) is constant and given by:

\[ \mathbf{H}_{\text{inside}} = -\frac{1}{3} \vec{M}. \] (1)

This result is derived from solving the boundary conditions at the surface of the sphere and using the fact that the magnetization \( \mathbf{M} \) is uniform. And the magnetic field \( \mathbf{B} \) can be calculated by:

\[ \mathbf{B}=\mu_0(\vec{H}+\vec{M})=\frac{2}{3}\mu_0\vec{M} \] (2)

To calculate the magnetic field outside the spherical magnet, the magnet can be approximated as a magnetic dipole. For a magnetic dipole, the magnetic dipole moment is expressed as:

\[ \vec{m}=\int_v\vec{M}dV, \] (3)

where \(\mathbf{M}\) is the magnetization and V is the volume of the bead.


Then, the magnetic scalar potential \( \mathbf{\Phi}_m \) can be derived:

\[ \Phi_m(r)=\frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int_{v'}\frac{\vec{M}\cdot \hat{r}'}{|\vec{r}-\vec{r}'|^2}dv'. \] (4)

For a spherical magnet located at the origin, Eq. (4) can be simplified to:

\[ \Phi_m(r)=\frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\frac{\vec{m}\cdot \hat{r}}{r^2}, \] (5)

where:

  • \( \mu_0 \): Vacuum permeability.
  • \( \vec{m} \): Magnetic dipole moment of the bead.
  • \( \hat{r} \): Unit vector in the direction of \( \vec{r} \).
  • \( r \): Distance from the dipole center to the field point.

Based on this, the magnetic field \( \mathbf{B} \) at a point \( \mathbf{r} \) in space is given by:

\[ \mathbf{B}=-\nabla\Phi_m=\frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\left [\frac{3(\vec{m}\cdot \hat{r})\hat{r}-\vec{m}}{ r^3}\right]. \] (6)

Magnetic Field of Multiple Magnetic Beads

The magnetic field intensity distribution for multiple magnetic beads can be calculated using the superposition principle. For multiple magnetic beads, the total magnetic field at a point is the vector sum of the fields generated by each bead:

\[ \mathbf{B}_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^N \mathbf{B}_i(\mathbf{r}), \] (7)

where \( N \) is the number of beads, and \( \mathbf{B}_i \) is the field from the \( i \)-th bead, calculated using the dipole formula.


As an example, the case of two magnetic beads is considered here. Consider two magnetic beads with dipole moments \( \mathbf{m}_1 \) and \( \mathbf{m}_2 \), located at positions \( \mathbf{r}_1 \) and \( \mathbf{r}_2 \), respectively. The field at a point \( \mathbf{r} \) is:

\[ \mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r}) = \mathbf{B}_1(\mathbf{r}) + \mathbf{B}_2(\mathbf{r}), \] (8)

where each term is computed using Eq. (6), with \( \mathbf{r}_i \) replaced by the vector \( \mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_i \).


Suppose that the two spherical magnets are located at \((0,r,0)\) and \((0,-r,0)\), symmetric about the y axis respectively. And the magnetic dipole moment is:

\[ \vec{m}=(0,m,0). \] (9)

Then, the total magnetic field of two beads cases can be calculated from:

\[ \mathbf{B}_1 = \frac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi r_{1}^{3}}\left( \frac{3(y-r)mx}{r_{1}^{2}}, \frac{3(y-r)^{2}m}{r_{1}^{2}} - m, \frac{3(y-r)mz}{r_{1}^{2}} \right), \] (10)
\[ \mathbf{B}_2 = \frac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi r_{2}^{3}}\left( \frac{3(y+r)mx}{r_{2}^{2}}, \frac{3(y+r)^{2}m}{r_{2}^{2}} - m, \frac{3(y+r)mz}{r_{2}^{2}} \right), \] (11)

where \( r_1 \) and \( r_2 \) are the Euclidean distance between a random point \((x,y,z)\) in the space and the two magnet beads,

\[ r_1=\sqrt{x^{2}+(y-r)^{2}+z^{2}}, \] (12)
\[ r_2=\sqrt{x^{2}+(y+r)^{2}+z^{2}}. \] (13)

Experiment Process

Experiment Display

Experiment Setup

To investigate the relationship between the device’s output voltage and the magnets and the coil counts, we built a test system consisting of a motor, a circular rotating disk, and an oscilloscope.

Fig. 2. Experimental setup: top is the schematic diagram and bottom is the physical prototype.


Simulation Results

Fig. 3. Magnetic field distribution of uniform magnetized: (a) cylindrical magnet, (b) multiple spherical magnets.

Fig. 4. COMSOL simulation results of the the output characteristics tested under open circuit situation: (a) peak power, and (b) average power of the output.


In order to better analyze the output characteristics of the proposed energy harvester, MATLAB and COMSOL simulation were conducted respectively to model the magnetic distribution of spherical magnets and the magnetic line cutting motion between magnets and coils. Fig. 2 illustrates that the magnetic field of multiple magnets can be equivalent to that of a single cylindrical magnet while analyzing. According to the COMSOL simulation results shown in Fig. 3, output of the harvester exhibits a positive correlation with the number of magnets and coil turns.

Experiment Results

Fig. 5. Experiment results of the output characteristics tested under a 300 𝛺 load resistor: (a) peak power, and (b) average power of the output.

Fig. 6. Experiment results: (a) capacitor charging experiment, and (b) LED lighting experiment.


Output characteristic of our design was tested through varing coil counts from 1 to 5, and the number of magnets from 2 to 6. The maximum output voltage and average output power showed good agreement with simulation results, exhibiting a positive correlation with the number of magnetic beads and coil turns, and achieving a maximum peak power of 23 mW according to Fig. 5.


To evaluate the charging capability of the device, capacitor charging experiments were conducted on 100 μF, 220 μF, and 330 μF capacitors respectively, using a full-wave rectifier to convert the AC output to DC while rotating. The 100 μF, 230 μF and 330 μF capacitors were charged to 1.75 V, 1.625 V, and 1.275 V in 180 seconds respectively, demonstrating a high energy harvesting efficiency and storage performance.


To further prove the performance of our design, a LED lighting experiment was conducted. As illustrated in Fig. 7, our harvester successfully powered 107 LEDs in parallel.
